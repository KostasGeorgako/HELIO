Part 1: What wavelengths actually tell you (plain physics)
Think of the spectrum as a fingerprint of what's on the ground. Different materials absorb and reflect light differently at different wavelengths. That's all this is.
Here's the full 420–2445 nm range split into regions that actually mean something:
420–700 nm — Visible light (what your eyes see)

Healthy green plants absorb blue (~450 nm) and red (~670 nm) for photosynthesis
They reflect green (~550 nm) — that's why they look green
Dead/dry plants stop absorbing → they look brown/yellow

700–900 nm — Red edge + Near-Infrared (NIR)

Healthy plants reflect NIR very strongly (cell structure, not pigment)
This is the single most important region for vegetation
The "red edge" at ~700–730 nm is the sharp jump from low red reflectance to high NIR — its position and slope tells you biomass and chlorophyll concentration
Dead or stressed plants → the jump is weaker and shifts

1000–1800 nm — SWIR1 (Shortwave Infrared)

Leaf and soil water content (absorbs around 1200 nm, 1450 nm)
Soil mineral content
The 1340–1460 nm window is mostly atmospheric water vapour absorbing the signal before it reaches the satellite — you get noise, not ground truth. Drop it.

1800–2450 nm — SWIR2

Clay minerals have a specific absorption feature at ~2200 nm (OH bend)
Carbonates at ~2330 nm
Organic matter, salt
Again, 1790–1960 nm is another atmospheric water vapour window. Drop it.

850–1000 nm — the stitch zone

EnMAP has two physical detectors: one for VNIR, one for SWIR. They join here.
The join creates oscillation artefacts (noise, not real signal). Drop it too.

Summary — what to keep vs drop:
KEEP:  420–850 nm        (VNIR — vegetation, chlorophyll, soil)
DROP:  850–1000 nm       (detector stitch noise)
KEEP:  1000–1340 nm      (SWIR — moisture, soil minerals)
DROP:  1340–1460 nm      (water vapour — atmosphere eating signal)
KEEP:  1460–1790 nm      (SWIR — clay, organic, dry soil)
DROP:  1790–1960 nm      (water vapour again)
KEEP:  1960–2445 nm      (SWIR2 — clay at 2200, carbonate at 2330)
After dropping, you have ~180–195 usable bands out of 224.

Part 2: What the indices are and why they matter for this competition
These are simple arithmetic on specific bands. They convert a 224-point spectrum into a single number that means something physically.
NDVI = (NIR₈₆₀ − Red₆₆₀) / (NIR₈₆₀ + Red₆₆₀)
→ Ranges −1 to +1. Bare soil ≈ 0.1. Sparse vegetation ≈ 0.3. Dense healthy crops ≈ 0.6–0.8.
→ Investment meaning: high NDVI = productive agricultural land.
NDRE = (NIR₇₈₀ − RedEdge₇₂₀) / (NIR₇₈₀ + RedEdge₇₂₀)
→ Same idea but uses the red-edge. More sensitive than NDVI at high biomass levels where NDVI saturates.
→ Use both. If NDRE is high but NDVI is saturated, it confirms genuinely dense vegetation.
NDMI = (NIR₈₆₀ − SWIR₁₆₄₀) / (NIR₈₆₀ + SWIR₁₆₄₀)
→ Leaf moisture content. High = wet leaves = active growing plants or irrigated land.
→ Investment meaning: chronically low NDMI = dry/rain-fed land with stress risk.
BSI (Bare Soil Index) = ((SWIR₁₆₀₀ + Red₆₆₀) − (NIR₈₃₀ + Blue₄₈₀)) / (...)
→ High = lot of exposed bare soil = low vegetation cover or degraded land.
Clay Index = ratio around 2200 nm absorption depth
→ High = clay-rich soil. Clay soils swell when wet, crack when dry — expensive for foundations and problematic for certain crops.
→ Investment meaning: very high clay = hidden risk, not a plus.
RX Anomaly Score — Mahalanobis distance of each pixel from the "average pixel" of all four sites pooled together.
→ High score = this pixel looks nothing like typical agricultural land. Could be: contamination, buried infrastructure, salinity patch, wetland.
→ Investment meaning: lots of anomalous pixels = hidden problem.

Savitzky-Golay Smoothing
Imagine you measure temperature every second for an hour but your thermometer has random jitter — the real temperature is a smooth curve but your readings jump up and down slightly around it. You want to remove the jitter without distorting the real trend.
Simple moving average would work, but it smears everything — a sharp peak in your data gets flattened and shifted because you're just averaging neighbours equally.
Savitzky-Golay does something smarter: instead of averaging, it takes a small window of consecutive points (say 9 bands) and fits a polynomial curve through them using least squares. Then it replaces the centre point with what the polynomial predicts there. Slide the window one step, repeat.
The key insight is that real spectral features have smooth polynomial shape — the red-edge is an S-curve, absorption dips are smooth valleys. Noise is random and doesn't fit a polynomial. So fitting a polynomial through a local window naturally keeps the real shape and discards the random jitter.
Visually, if your raw spectrum looks like this:
Real signal + noise:   /\/\/\___/\/\/\___
After SG smoothing:    /      \_/        \___
The two parameters you set are:

Window length (we use 9 bands) — how many neighbouring bands to include. Too small = doesn't smooth enough. Too large = starts distorting real features.
Polynomial order (we use 2) — degree of the polynomial fitted. Order 2 = parabola. Higher order follows sharper features but also follows noise more.

For hyperspectral data at EnMAP's resolution (224 bands over 2000nm), window=9 and order=2 is standard — it removes instrument noise while keeping the red-edge slope, water absorption dips, and clay features intact.

How to Read the Script 02 Plots Yourself
The spatial index maps (idx_NDVI_maps.png, idx_NDRE_maps.png, etc.)
Each file shows four panels side by side — one per site. Each panel is an 18×18 grid of pixels where colour = index value. Grey pixels = nodata (border edge pixels you already know about).
What to look at in any index map:
1. Overall colour — is the site warm or cool on the scale?
The colorbar is on the right. Find where most of your site's pixels sit on that scale. For NDVI with RdYlGn (red=bad, green=good): a mostly green panel = healthy vegetation. Mostly red = bare soil.
2. Uniformity — is the colour consistent or patchy?
A site where every pixel is the same shade = uniform land. A site with half dark-red and half bright-green pixels = mixed land use, higher risk. This is one of your investment axes.
3. The number under each panel — μ and σ
μ (mu) = mean value across all valid pixels = the average condition of the plot.
σ (sigma) = standard deviation = how spread out the pixel values are. Large σ = heterogeneous plot.
4. Grey spatial pattern — where are the anomalies?
Even before running the ML detector, index maps often show suspicious isolated pixels — one bright yellow pixel in a sea of green, or a cluster of red in an otherwise healthy NDVI map. Note where those are. The anomaly script will quantify them.

Tall bar + long error bar = site has this property on average but some pixels deviate a lot (mixed)

The uniformity CV chart (uniformity_cv.png)
CV = Coefficient of Variation = σ/μ. It answers: "relative to the average value, how much do pixels vary?"
A site with NDVI mean=0.6 and σ=0.1 has CV=0.17 (17% variation).
A site with NDVI mean=0.2 and σ=0.1 has CV=0.50 (50% variation — same absolute spread but much more significant relative to the low mean).
Lower CV = more uniform = better investment (predictable land behaves predictably).
Read it as: which bar is shortest? That site is most internally consistent for that index. If Magnisia has the tallest NDVI bar in this chart, it confirms what we saw visually — the white patches make it spatially chaotic.
